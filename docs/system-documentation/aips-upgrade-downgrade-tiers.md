How should I price  upgrades and downgrades? Should I discount for previous tier already paid?

Great question — this is one of the more nuanced decisions in SaaS pricing. Here's how to think through it:

---

## The Standard Approach: Prorate It

The most common and customer-friendly approach is **prorated billing** — charge only for the remaining time in their billing period at the new tier's rate, minus the unused value of their current plan.

**Example:** A Standard user ($49/yr) upgrades to Premium ($199/yr) 6 months in.
- Unused Standard value: ~$24.50
- Premium for remaining 6 months: ~$99.50
- **Charge at upgrade: ~$75**

This feels fair, transparent, and removes the "I just paid and now I'm paying again" objection. Most billing tools (Stripe, Paddle, etc.) handle this automatically.

---

## Should You Discount Beyond Proration?

Generally, **no** — and here's why:

- Proration already *is* the discount. The customer isn't double-paying, so there's no goodwill deficit to make up for.
- Additional discounts on upgrades can actually **cheapen perceived value** and train users to wait for deals.
- Your price gaps are meaningful ($49 → $199 → $499), so you want users to feel the tiers are worth their full price.

**The exception:** If a user is upgrading very early in their cycle (say, within the first 30 days), a small goodwill credit or a rounded-down charge can feel generous without being a slippery habit.

---

## Downgrade Timing: Don't Refund, Do Credit

For downgrades, the cleanest approach is:

- **No cash refunds** — keep it as account credit toward their next renewal
- **Access continues** until the current period ends, then the lower tier kicks in
- This protects your revenue while still feeling fair to the customer

---

## A Simple Framework for Your Three Tiers

| Scenario | Recommended Approach |
|---|---|
| Standard → Premium mid-year | Prorate the difference |
| Standard → Professional mid-year | Prorate the difference |
| Premium → Professional mid-year | Prorate the difference |
| Any tier → downgrade | No refund; access until period ends |
| Upgrade within first 30 days | Consider rounding down to nearest $5–$10 as a goodwill gesture |

---

## One Thing to Be Explicit About on Your Pricing Page

State your upgrade/downgrade policy clearly — something like:

> *"Upgrades are prorated to the day. Downgrades take effect at your next renewal with no partial refunds."*

Customers who understand the policy upfront rarely complain about it. Surprise is the real churn driver, not the policy itself.
